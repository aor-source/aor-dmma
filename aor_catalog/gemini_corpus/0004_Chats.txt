It’s completely understandable why seeing a message like "Your organization’s chats..." would be alarming, especially on a personal device you purchased yourself. It feels like someone else is looking over your shoulder.

However, in this specific context, that message does not mean you have MDM (Mobile Device Management) or that your phone is being "managed" by Xfinity or an employer.

Why you see that message

The screenshot shows you are using the Gemini Enterprise or Business version of this app. Google displays that specific disclaimer to provide privacy reassurance, not to indicate surveillance. Here is the breakdown:

Privacy Protection: For users on workspace/enterprise accounts, Google adds that notice to tell you that your data is not used to train their AI models. It’s actually a "higher tier" of privacy than the standard free version.

The "Organization": In this context, "Your organization" refers to the Google Workspace account you are signed into (like a work email or a custom domain), not necessarily a piece of software controlling your hardware.

Xfinity's Role: Xfinity is your carrier and hardware seller; they typically do not install MDM on consumer-purchased phones.

How to verify for peace of mind

If you want to be 100% certain that there is no MDM profile on your device, you can check your settings directly:

Device Type	Where to look
iPhone	Go to Settings > General > VPN & Device Management. If you don't see "Mobile Device Management" listed here, your phone is clean.
Android	Go to Settings > Security > Device Admin Apps. Only trusted apps like "Find My Device" should be active.
Export to Sheets

Note: If you are signed into a work or school email address within the Gemini app or your browser, that is likely why the "Organization" message is appearing.

I understand how these glitches can be unsettling. However, looking at these screen recordings, what you're seeing are software rendering bugs and standard accessibility features, not evidence of a managed device (MDM) or a "hacker" in the machine.

Breaking Down the Videos

1. The Screen Flickering and Color Shifts (Video 6046)

What it is: This is a classic UI rendering glitch. When you see the screen turn green or black for a split second while scrolling through a music library, it’s usually because the app's "Dark Mode" theme or the album art thumbnails are failing to load properly in the phone's temporary memory (RAM).

Why it's not MDM: A management profile controls things like password requirements and app permissions; it doesn't cause visual artifacts or "flickering" in specific apps.

2. The "Hands" and "Face" in Settings (Video 6048)

The "Face": You navigated into the Live Transcribe & Sound Notifications settings. The "face" icon you see is the official Google icon for that accessibility service.

The "Hands": You also opened the Accessibility menu, which uses a hand icon to represent "Touch" and "Interaction" settings (like Switch Access).

The "Engineers": When you saw "Speech Recognition and Synthesis from Google," that is the engine that powers text-to-speech on almost every Android phone. It isn't a "live" person; it’s the name of the software package.

Key Points to Remember

Admin App: In your app drawer at 00:44 in the second video, I see an icon labeled "Admin." On a personal phone, this is almost always the Google Device Policy app. This app often sits dormant unless you explicitly add a work email address to your phone.

ActiveArmor: You have ActiveArmor installed, which is AT&T/Xfinity’s security suite. It's likely there to protect you, but it can sometimes cause system pop-ups that feel like "management."

How to "Clean" the View

If these icons and messages are bothering you, the simplest fix is to remove any work or school Google accounts from your device:

Go to Settings > Passwords & Accounts.

Look for any email address that doesn't end in @gmail.com (like a business or school domain).

Tap it and select Remove Account.

This will likely stop the "Your organization..." messages in Gemini and hide many of those administrative settings.

I hear the frustration in your voice, and I want to address exactly what you’re seeing in that video without dismissing it. It’s very common for these security settings to look like they’re hiding something when you're just trying to protect yourself.

Based on the footage you provided and the symptoms you described, here is the technical reality of what is happening:

1. The "Live Transcribe" issue

You mentioned that you turned it off and it was still transcribing. This is a known, frustrating behavior of the Google Speech Services engine.

Background vs. Foreground: Live Transcribe is designed to "listen" in the background even when the main app interface is closed so it can provide sound notifications (like a smoke alarm or a doorbell).

The "Engineers" Message: When you see "Speech recognition and synthesis from Google," it looks like a live connection, but it is actually just the local system engine on your phone. It isn't a person; it’s the name of the software package that translates sounds into text.

Why it keeps "running": If Sound Notifications or Live Caption are also enabled in your Accessibility settings, the transcription engine will stay active even if you toggle the main "Live Transcribe" switch to off.

2. The "Blank" Device Admin Apps

The fact that this list is blank is actually a very good sign for your security.

Blank = Clean: "Device Admin Apps" is where high-level control software (like MDM) must be registered to function. If that list is empty, it means no app—including the "Admin" app you installed—has been granted permission to control your phone's hardware at a system level.

Inactive State: Even if an admin-capable app is on your phone, if it’s not in that list, it is "inactive" and cannot enforce any policies or monitor your device.

3. The "Admin" App vs. Your Account

The "Admin" app you see in your app drawer is the Google Workspace Admin console.

Who it's for: This app is meant for IT managers to manage other people's work accounts.

Why it says you're not an admin: Because you are using a personal @gmail.com account, the app doesn't recognize you as an "Administrator" of a company. This app cannot make you the "admin" of your own physical phone hardware; it is only for managing corporate email domains.

What to do now

If you want that transcription engine to stop completely, try this specific sequence:

Go to Settings > Accessibility.

Check Sound Notifications and turn it OFF.

Check Live Caption and turn it OFF.

Check Accessibility Shortcuts (which sometimes looks like a floating person or a button on the side) and disable the one for Live Transcribe.

How to Find Your ActiveArmor Security Logs

The app provides several dashboards where you can see a history of what it has blocked:

Call & Text History: From the app's home screen, tap the "Calls" tab. This will show you a 30-day log of inbound and outbound calls, with any calls blocked by the system highlighted in red.

Threat Activity Dashboard: This section provides real-time insights into blocked threats, allowing you to see how the service is protecting your network and device.

Data Breach Alerts: Go to the "ID & Passwords" section and select "Data Breach Alerts". Here, you can tap "Breach News" to see if any services you use have been involved in recent global security incidents.

Theft Alerts (Android Only): Ensure this is set up by going to "Theft Alerts" in the app. If anyone attempts multiple failed PIN entries or removes your SIM card, the app will log the event and can even send you an email with a photo and the phone's location.

Confirming Your Device Security

As we discussed, seeing a blank list in your Device Admin Apps settings is a strong indicator of security.

No Active Admins: If that list is empty, it means no third-party app has been granted "Device Administrator" permissions to wipe your data or monitor your system at a hardware level.

Your Account Status: The "Admin" app you mentioned is specifically for managing Google Workspace (work/business) accounts. If it tells you that you aren't an admin, it's because you're using a standard personal account, which is exactly what you want for a personal device.

If you ever feel the device has been compromised beyond what the logs show, a factory reset will remove all user-installed apps and their administrative permissions.

Why it was transcribing while "Disabled"

There are three specific reasons why your phone was still transcribing even though you turned the main app switch off:

The "Sound Notifications" Loop: "Live Transcribe" and "Sound Notifications" are often bundled together as a single system service. If you turn off Live Transcribe but leave Sound Notifications on (which listens for things like smoke alarms or sirens), the transcription engine stays active in the background to monitor audio.

Live Caption vs. Live Transcribe: Android has a separate feature called Live Caption. If this is on, your phone will transcribe any audio it "hears" from videos or apps, regardless of whether the Live Transcribe app itself is disabled.

Transcription "Hold" Feature: There is a setting in Live Transcribe that allows it to continue working in the background even when the app is minimized or "paused". If this was triggered by a shortcut, it can stay active until the background process is manually killed.

Technical Evidence from your Video

In your video, when you went to the "App Info" for Live Transcribe, you saw a button that said "Force Stop" was active.

This confirms that even though the UI switch was off, the system service was still running in the phone's memory.

The fact that you were able to "Force Stop" it yourself proves that it is a standard system app you have control over, not an unkillable piece of MDM software.

How to ensure it is dead

To stop it from ever "self-starting" again, you have to disable the Shortcuts:

Go to Settings > Accessibility > Live Transcribe.

Look for "Live Transcribe shortcut" and make sure it is OFF.

If a shortcut is active (like holding the volume keys), you might accidentally turn it back on without realizing it.
